Q1.1: The linguistic conflict is between a default prohibition and a conditional permission. The phrase "shall not transfer" frames cross-border transfer as prohibited unless another rule applies. The phrase "except in accordance with requirements prescribed under this Act" then creates an exception pathway. The conflict is not that the phrases cancel each other; it is that they point in opposite regulatory directions. One reads as restrictive by default, while the other allows transfer if conditions are met. The phrase "comparable protection" adds ambiguity because it does not itself say whether compliance means an adequacy decision, contractual safeguards, case-by-case approval, consent, or another mechanism.

Q1.2: We treat the prohibition as the primary rule and the exception as a modifier. In legal drafting, an exception clause normally narrows the operation of the main rule but does not erase it. The default position is therefore that transfer is restricted unless the prescribed requirements are satisfied. For RDTII scoring, this matters because the compliance cost comes from the conditional regime: an organisation must determine whether the destination and transfer mechanism meet the statutory standard. We score the primary clause as restrictive or conditional, then record the exception as the pathway that reduces the severity from an absolute ban to a conditional transfer regime.

Q1.3: Our system does not ask the model to freely interpret the whole clause. The constrained prompt requires the model to identify evidence for one RDTII indicator at a time and quote the exact supporting phrase. For this clause, the model must handle the primary rule and exception separately: first the "shall not transfer" restriction, then the "except in accordance with requirements" condition. Vague terms such as "comparable protection" are flagged as UNCERTAIN if the source text does not define them. The No Quote, No Score rule prevents the model from blending both phrases into a synthesized conclusion without a verbatim citation.
